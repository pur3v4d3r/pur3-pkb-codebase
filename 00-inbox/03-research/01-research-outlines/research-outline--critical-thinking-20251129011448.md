## ðŸ§­ Learning Path Architecture

> [!principle-point] Sequencing Rationale
> This curriculum follows Bloom's hierarchical framework where each stage of learning is a prerequisite for the next stage. We begin with conceptual foundations (understanding what CT IS and its cognitive architecture), progress to the structural elements (Paul-Elder's Elements of Thought), then build evaluative capabilities (Intellectual Standards and bias recognition), develop metacognitive awareness (self-regulation and intellectual traits), and finally apply these integrated skills to academic knowledge work and PKB curation. This mirrors how critical thinking converts raw data and facts into meaningful understanding and insight through systematic skill development.

### Phase 1: Foundation Building (Weeks 1-3)
Establishing conceptual framework and cognitive architecture of critical thinking

### Phase 2: Core Standards & Skills Development (Weeks 4-7)  
Building analytical and evaluative capabilities through systematic practice

### Phase 3: Integration & Metacognitive Development (Weeks 8-10)
Developing self-awareness and intellectual character traits

### Phase 4: Application to Academic Knowledge Work (Weeks 11-12)
Applying CT skills to research, synthesis, and PKB integration

### Topic 2: Paul-Elder Framework Part 1 - The Elements of Thought

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Master the 8 Elements of Thought/Reasoning that structure all thinking
> - Apply the Elements framework to analyze your own reasoning processes
> - Use the Elements to deconstruct arguments, texts, and claims systematically
> - Recognize how the Elements interrelate to form coherent reasoning

**Pedagogical Approach**: **First-Principles**  
**Rationale**: The Elements of Thought are the fundamental "parts" of thinking that students need to master. This first-principles approach treats the 8 Elements as axioms of reasoning - every instance of thought contains these components. By mastering these foundational building blocks, you can systematically analyze and improve any reasoning process.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What is the Question at Issue we're trying to answer?
2. What is my Purpose in this thinking? What am I trying to accomplish?
3. What Information (data/evidence) am I using to make my decision?
4. What Assumptions am I making (taking for granted)?
5. What Inferences and Interpretations am I drawing from the information?
6. What Concepts (theories, principles, models) am I employing to understand this?
7. What are the Implications and Consequences of my reasoning?
8. What is my Point of View, and how might alternative perspectives see this differently?

### Topic 3: Paul-Elder Framework Part 2 - Intellectual Standards

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Learn the 9 Universal Intellectual Standards for evaluating reasoning quality
> - Apply standards systematically to assess arguments, claims, and your own thinking
> - Develop the habit of asking standard-based questions (e.g., "Is this clear?", "Is this accurate?", "Is this relevant?")
> - Recognize when reasoning fails to meet appropriate standards

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Analytical**  
**Rationale**: The intellectual standards are used to determine the quality of reasoning. This analytical approach teaches you to evaluate thinking rigorously using explicit criteria. Standards transform CT from vague "good thinking" into systematic quality assessment with specific benchmarks.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. Is my thinking Clear - easy to understand and well-explained?
2. Is it Accurate - correct and free from error?
3. Is it Precise - exact with sufficient detail?
4. Is it Relevant - connected to the question at hand?
5. Does it have Depth - dealing with complexities adequately?
6. Does it have Breadth - considering other viewpoints?
7. Is it Logical - making sense step-by-step with conclusions following from evidence?
8. Does it address what is Significant - focusing on the most important ideas?
9. Is it Fair - balanced and free from bias?

### Topic 4: Cognitive Biases and Logical Fallacies

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Identify common cognitive biases that distort reasoning (confirmation bias, availability heuristic, anchoring, etc.)
> - Recognize logical fallacies in arguments (ad hominem, straw man, false dichotomy, slippery slope, etc.)
> - Understand the psychological mechanisms behind biased thinking
> - Develop strategies to mitigate bias in your own thinking and recognize it in others

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Problem-Solving**  
**Rationale**: Biases and fallacies represent systematic errors in thinking. A problem-solving approach allows you to diagnose these errors as specific problems with identifiable solutions. By treating bias recognition as a debugging process, you develop practical skills for improving reasoning quality in real-world contexts.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What are the most prevalent cognitive biases affecting academic knowledge acquisition?
2. How do System 1 (fast, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, deliberate) thinking contribute to bias?
3. Why are we susceptible to logical fallacies despite knowing about them?
4. How can I design my PKB system and note-taking practices to counter cognitive biases?
5. What is the relationship between egocentric/sociocentric thinking and bias?
