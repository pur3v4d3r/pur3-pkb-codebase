**Core Question**: "What is the 'Socratic Fallacy' that critics attribute to Socrates' definitional method, and does this critique reveal genuine logical flaws or misunderstand Socratic inquiry's purpose?"

**Why This Topic is Foundationally Rich**:
The **Socratic Fallacy**, as identified by philosopher **Peter Geach**, charges that Socrates commits a logical error by insisting one cannot know *instances* of a concept (e.g., particular courageous acts) without first knowing the *definition* of the concept itself (what courage *is*). This topic examines the critique: Geach argues we often correctly identify examples of X without possessing an explicit definition of X‚Äîyou can recognize dogs without defining "dogness." The topic explores whether this is a genuine fallacy or a misreading of Socratic epistemology, analyzes the relationship between ostensive and stipulative definitions, and connects to modern debates in philosophy of language (Wittgenstein's "family resemblance," prototype theory in cognitive science). It also examines the implications: if the Socratic Fallacy is real, does it undermine the entire elenctic project?
