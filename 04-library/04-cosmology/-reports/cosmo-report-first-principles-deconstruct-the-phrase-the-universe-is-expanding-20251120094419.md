> [!abstract]
> The phrase "the universe is expanding" carries conceptual baggage that obscures its true meaning. When most peopleâ€”including many science enthusiastsâ€”hear this statement, they construct a mental picture borrowed wholesale from everyday experience. # 2.0 âš›ï¸ THE ATOMS: Identifying the First Principles

This is the most critical section of our deconstruction. Here, we completely ignore the "Explosion Through Space" model from Section 1.0 and ask: **"What are the *fundamental, indisputable truths* about the universe that we can establish through direct observation?"** We are looking for the "atoms" of the problemâ€”the raw observables, the measured quantities, the repeatable experimental facts that exist independent of our theories or interpretations. These are the data points from which we must construct any explanation.

> [!question]
> - **Stripping away all assumptions, what is the *fundamental problem* we are *actually* trying to solve?**
>   - The fundamental problem is not "why are galaxies moving through space?" Rather, it is: **"Why does light from distant galaxies arrive at Earth with its wavelength stretched toward longer, redder wavelengths, and why does this stretching increase systematically with distance?"** This is the observable fact that demands explanation. Everything elseâ€”expansion, Big Bang, spatial geometryâ€”represents our *interpretation* of this fact, not the fact itself.

### Observable Fact 1: [[Cosmological Redshift]]

The first indisputable truth is observational: when we analyze light from distant galaxies using spectroscopy, we find that characteristic spectral linesâ€”the fingerprints of specific atoms like hydrogen, helium, and carbonâ€”are shifted toward longer wavelengths compared to the same spectral lines measured in terrestrial laboratories. This wavelength shift, denoted mathematically as [[redshift]] $z$, is defined precisely:

$$z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{emitted}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}}$$

where $\lambda_{\text{observed}}$ is the wavelength we measure here on Earth, and $\lambda_{\text{emitted}}$ is the wavelength the atom would emit in a laboratory. This shift results from the expansion of space itself, not from galaxies moving through space. A photon emitted billions of years ago with a wavelength of 500 nanometers (green light) might arrive today with a wavelength of 1000 nanometers (infrared), having been stretched by a factor of two during its journey.

The observations show that redshifts are larger for more distant galaxies, following a pattern where the cosmological redshift can be written as a function of the cosmic scale factor, which increases monotonically with time. This relationship is not merely qualitativeâ€”it is quantitative, systematic, and precisely measurable. Galaxies at distances of 100 million light-years show small redshifts ($z \approx 0.007$), while galaxies at billions of light-years show enormous redshifts ($z > 1$, meaning wavelengths more than doubled).

**Critically**, this is not a [[Doppler shift]] in the conventional sense. Cosmological redshift results from the expansion of space itself and not from the motion of individual objects through space, with the wavelength lengthening as light travels through expanding space. A Doppler shift depends on the velocity of the source *at the moment of emission*; cosmological redshift depends on *how much space has expanded during the photon's entire journey*. The proper interpretation is that cosmological redshift is not a Doppler shift caused by motion of galaxies through space, but rather a stretching of light caused by the stretching of space as the photon travels.

### Observable Fact 2: [[Hubble's Law]] and Systematic Distance-Redshift Correlation

The second atomic truth builds on the first: Edwin Hubble's observations showed that the recession velocity of galaxies is directly proportional to their distance, a relationship now known as Hubble's law. This relationship can be expressed mathematically as:

$$v = H_0 \times d$$

where $v$ represents the recession velocity, $d$ is the distance to the galaxy, and $H_0$ is the [[Hubble constant]]â€”approximately 70 kilometers per second per megaparsec. This means that for every megaparsec (roughly 3.26 million light-years) of distance, the apparent recession velocity increases by 70 km/s.

Building on earlier redshift measurements by Vesto Slipher, Hubble used precise distance and redshift data to quantify this relationship, providing the observational basis for the Big Bang theory. This is not a scattered, random patternâ€”it is a *law-like* regularity. Double the distance, double the redshift. Triple the distance, triple the redshift. This systematic correlation demands an explanation that goes beyond mere coincidence.

What makes this observation profound is its **isotropy**â€”it holds in every direction we look. Observations to date support the idea that the universe is both isotropic (looking the same in all directions) and homogeneous (having the same average density everywhere on large scales). We do not see galaxies approaching from one hemisphere and receding in another. We do not see a preferred axis or direction. In every direction, at every comparable distance, we measure comparable redshifts.

### Observable Fact 3: The [[Cosmic Microwave Background]] Temperature Evolution

The third fundamental observable provides independent confirmation of cosmic expansion through an entirely different physical mechanism: the temperature of the [[cosmic microwave background radiation]]. The cosmic microwave background is microwave radiation that fills all space in the observable universe, currently measured at a temperature of 2.725 K, and represents radiation left over from the early hot dense state.

The crucial evidence comes from measuring this background temperature at different redshiftsâ€”that is, at different cosmic epochs. Observations of submillimeter line absorption from water molecules in distant galaxies have measured the CMB temperature at redshift $z = 6.34$, finding temperatures of 16.4-30.2 K, consistent with the expected scaling where temperature increases with redshift. Earlier measurements detected absorption lines from neutral carbon atoms at redshift $z = 2.3371$, confirming that the CMB temperature was higher in the past.

The predicted relationship is that the CMB temperature at redshift $z$ should be $T_{\text{CMB}}(z) = T_{\text{CMB}}(0) \times (1 + z)$, where $T_{\text{CMB}}(0) = 2.72548 \pm 0.00057$ K is the current temperature. This is precisely what observations confirm: at $z = 1$, the temperature should be about 5.4 K; at $z = 2$, about 8.2 K; at $z = 6.34$, about 20 Kâ€”all matching observations within measurement uncertainty.

This temperature evolution is **not explained by galaxies moving through space**. Instead, the cosmic microwave background has a redshift of $z = 1089$, corresponding to a shift in average temperature from 3000 K down to 3 K, showing how photons from the early universe have been stretched along with expanding space. The photons haven't lost energy by doing work against gravity or colliding with matterâ€”they've been stretched by the expansion of space itself, cooling adiabatically as a consequence.

### Observable Fact 4: [[Cosmological Principle]]â€”Homogeneity and Isotropy

The fourth atomic truth concerns the *distribution* of matter and radiation across cosmic scales. The cosmological principle states that on sufficiently large scales, the universe is both homogeneous (the same everywhere) and isotropic (the same in all directions), with observational support from the cosmic microwave background and large-scale galaxy surveys.

Homogeneity means there is no preferred observing position in the universeâ€”the same observational evidence is available to observers at different locations. Isotropy means the same observational evidence is available by looking in any direction, with isotropy implying homogeneity but not vice versa.

This principle has profound implications: If the universe is homogeneous and isotropic, then it has no edge (as this would violate homogeneity) and no center (as this would violate isotropy). Most cosmologists believe that the universe is infinite, with nothing outside of it, as the expansion of space on large scales is not enough to be halted by gravity due to the inverse square law.

The observational evidence for this principle is compelling. The clearest modern evidence comes from measurements of the cosmic microwave background, whose nearly perfect isotropy (uniform appearance in all directions after removing local motion effects) reflects the homogeneity of the early universe. On scales larger than several hundred megaparsecs, galaxy surveys confirm that matter distribution becomes increasingly uniform, though local structures (galaxies, clusters, superclusters) dominate on smaller scales.

> [!principle-point]
> - **First Principle 1:** [[Raw Redshift Data]]
>   - Light from distant galaxies arrives with wavelengths systematically stretched toward the red end of the spectrum. > [!principle-point]

>   - The cosmic microwave background radiationâ€”a nearly uniform bath of photons filling all of spaceâ€”has a temperature that varies systematically with cosmic epoch. > [!principle-point]
> - **First Principle 3:** [[Homogeneity and Isotropy]]
>   - When averaged over sufficiently large scales (hundreds of millions of light-years), the universe displays no preferred direction and no preferred location. > [!principle-point]
> - **First Principle 4:** [[Finite Light Speed]] and [[Lookback Time]]
>   - Light travels at a finite, constant speed ($c = 299,792,458$ m/s in vacuum). This means that observing distant objects necessarily means observing the *past*. A galaxy 1 billion light-years away is seen as it was 1 billion years ago. This creates a fundamental asymmetry: we can observe the past (by looking at distant objects) but not the future. Combined with systematic redshift patterns, this implies the universe has a *history*â€”it evolves over time.

> [!summary]
> **Our "Atomic" Truths:**
> We have established exactly four fundamental observables: (1) **Systematic redshift increasing with distance**, measured directly from spectroscopy; (2) **CMB temperature increasing toward the past**, measured from absorption lines in distant clouds; (3) **Homogeneity and isotropy at large scales**, measured from galaxy surveys and CMB maps; (4) **Finite light speed creating lookback time**, established by special relativity and countless experiments.
