### **On the Two-Vault Approach: Separation vs. Integration**

Your initial thought to create two separate, independent vaults—one for your *Personal* life and one for a *Reference Library*—is a very common and intuitive starting point. It comes from a desire for order, for a clean separation between what is "mine" (my thoughts, my plans, my daily life) and what is "theirs" (the knowledge and ideas of others from books, articles, etc.). This impulse for clarity is absolutely a good thing.

**My Thoughts on This Approach:**

The two-vault system creates a strong, clear boundary. Your personal vault would remain a focused space for your daily actions, projects, and reflections, free from the "clutter" of a massive reference library. The reference vault would be a pure repository of knowledge. This can feel very organized and manageable at the outset.

**Would I Do It Differently? And Why?**

Yes, I would humbly suggest a different path. While the separation of two vaults provides clarity, it comes at a significant cost: **friction and the loss of serendipity.**

Think about the nature of a truly insightful idea. Does it arise in a vacuum? Rarely. More often, it is born from the unexpected collision of different domains of thought. A line from a philosophy book you're reading might suddenly illuminate a personal struggle you wrote about in your daily note. A technique from a photography article might spark an idea for a project you're planning.

This is the core magic of tools like Obsidian: the ability to weave a web of connections between disparate ideas.

In a two-vault system, linking an idea from a book in your *Reference* vault to a project in your *Personal* vault is impossible without cumbersome workarounds (like copying links or re-writing notes). That small amount of friction is often enough to prevent the connection from ever being made. The two vaults become silos, unable to speak to one another. Your thoughts and the knowledge that fuels them are kept apart, when their true power lies in their integration.

The goal, as I see it, is not to build a library separate from your life. The goal is to build a life enriched and informed by a living, breathing library that is interwoven with your daily experience.

Consider Glenn Randall, whose philosophy you're exploring. His breathtaking photography isn't born from keeping his knowledge of meteorology, geology, and light in one box, and his personal trip planning and creative vision in another. No, his art is the direct result of the seamless *integration* of that deep knowledge with his immediate, personal experience in the landscape.

Therefore, I advocate for a **single, unified vault**. Within this one vault, we will use folders and other organizational tools to create the very "separation" and "clarity" you're seeking, but without sacrificing the crucial ability to connect everything to everything else.

### **The "Why" Behind Each Folder: A Deeper Look**

Let's understand the purpose, the soul, of each of these containers.

#### **00 \- ATLAS**

This is your command center, the map to your world. It holds the "meta" notes and files that support your entire system.

* **Templates:** A home for pre-formatted notes. For example, you could have a Daily Note Template, a Book Notes Template, or a New Project Template. This reduces friction and ensures consistency.  
* **Attachments:** When you drag an image or a PDF into Obsidian, it needs a place to live. By telling Obsidian to store all attachments here, you keep your other folders clean and focused on your notes (the .md files).  
* **Daily Notes:** The heart of your personal vault. This is your journal, your logbook, your starting point for each day. Placing it in the ATLAS keeps this daily, foundational practice at the top of your system.

#### **10 \- INBOX**

This is your digital landing pad. Any fleeting idea, quick note, or web clipping that you don't have time to process immediately goes here. It is a temporary holding area. The rule is to review and process your inbox regularly (daily or weekly), moving these notes to their proper homes in PROJECTS, AREAS, or RESOURCES and linking them appropriately. This keeps your mind clear, knowing that no idea will be lost.

#### **20 \- PROJECTS**

A project is a series of tasks linked to a specific, definable goal with an endpoint.

* *Examples:* Plan Weekend Trip to St. Augustine, Build a New Bookshelf, Master Long Exposure Seascapes.  
* **The Philosophy:** These are your active endeavors. They are about *doing*. When a project is complete, its entire folder is moved to the 50 \- ARCHIVE, giving you a wonderful sense of accomplishment and keeping your active workspace clean.

#### **30 \- AREAS**

An area is a sphere of activity with a standard to be maintained over time. It doesn't have an end date.

* *Examples:* Health & Fitness (an ongoing concern), Finances, and for you, most importantly, Photography.  
* **The Philosophy:** These folders represent the core domains of your life. Under Photography, you can build out a rich internal world.  
  * Gear: You could have a note for your Nikon D300S, detailing its settings, quirks, and maintenance. Another for your Nikkor 17-55mm f/2.8G.  
  * Locations: Notes on spots around Jacksonville—Sunrise at Jacksonville Beach Pier, Black Rock Beach, etc.  
  * Techniques: Notes on The Exposure Triangle, Using your K\&F ND Filters, Compositional Rules.

#### **40 \- RESOURCES**

This is your Reference Library, now fully integrated into your world. This is where you store notes on things you learn and find interesting, organized by topic.

* *Examples:*  
  * Books: A note for Glenn Randall \- The Art of Landscape Photography.  
  * Articles: Notes from web clippings about post-processing.  
  * People: A note for \[\[Glenn Randall\]\] himself, where you can link all his ideas and books you've read.  
  * Concepts: Notes on abstract ideas like \[\[Stoicism\]\] or \[\[The Psychology of Color\]\].  
* **The Power of Connection:** Now, imagine. In your project note \[\[Project \- Master Long Exposure Seascapes\]\], you can directly link to your notes on \[\[Using your K\&F ND Filters\]\] from AREAS/Photography/Techniques, and also link to inspiration from \[\[Glenn Randall \- The Art of Landscape Photography\]\] in your RESOURCES. When you look at the graph view, you will see a beautiful constellation of connections showing how your knowledge is directly fueling your actions. This is something two separate vaults could never give you.

#### **50 \- ARCHIVE**

This is your digital memory palace. When a project from the 20 \- PROJECTS folder is completed, or a note in AREAS is no longer relevant to your current life, you move it here. It's not deleted; it's simply stored away, accessible if you ever need it but out of your daily view.
