### **Phase 3: The Forge - Processing Information into Knowledge**

*   **Hierarchical Links:** These provide structure. You create them by linking from a broader topic to a more specific one. This is the primary function of MOCs.

**2. Maps of Content (MOCs)**

An MOC is simply a note that serves as a table of contents for a topic. It's your curated entry point into a subject.

*   **When to Create an MOC:** When you notice a cluster of notes forming around a theme (e.g., you have five or six notes related to the English Civil War), it's time to create `[[English Civil War MOC]]`.
*   **How to Structure it:** An MOC is a collection of links. You can structure it with headings to organize your thoughts. For example, your `[[English Civil War MOC]]` might have sections like `### Key Figures`, `### Major Battles`, and `### Political Consequences`, with links to your relevant Concept Notes under each. You can also link to other related MOCs.

This is the magic of the system. Over months and years, as you connect notes, you will open the Graph View in Obsidian and see your thinking visualized. You will spot unexpected clusters, bridges between topics you thought were separate, and gaps in your knowledge that suggest new avenues for research. ### **Phase 5: The Garden - Vault Hygiene and Long-Term Growth**

A knowledge base is not a write-once, read-never archive. It's a garden that needs tending.

*   **Weekly:** Spend 30 minutes reviewing the notes you created that week. Refine the language. Add a new link you missed. This keeps your recent knowledge fresh and well-integrated.
*   **Monthly:** Pick one MOC and review it. Does the structure still make sense? Are there new notes you should add? This high-level review helps with synthesis.

**2. Refactoring**

Your understanding is not static, and your notes shouldn't be either.

*   **Refining:** As you learn more, you might go back to an old note and add more nuance or a counter-argument.
*   **Splitting:** If you realize a Concept Note has grown too large and contains more than one idea, split it into two (or more) atomic notes.
*   **Merging:** If you have two notes that are saying almost the same thing, merge them into one stronger note.

**3. Principles for Longevity**

*   **Embrace Imperfection:** Don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good. It's better to have a messy note than no note at all. You can always refine it later.
*   **Trust the Process:** It will feel slow at first. But the value of a networked knowledge base grows exponentially. The 100th note you add is far more valuable than the 10th, because it has 99 other notes it can connect to.
*   **Make it Your Own:** This SOP is a starting point, not a straitjacket. Over time, you will adapt it. Change the folder structure, modify the templates, and find the rhythm that works for you. The best system is the one you actually use.
