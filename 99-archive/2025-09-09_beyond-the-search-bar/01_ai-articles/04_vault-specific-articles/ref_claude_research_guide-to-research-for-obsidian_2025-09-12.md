## Module 1: Mathematical and Conceptual Foundations (Weeks 1-4)

### Core mathematical prerequisites

**Linear algebra fundamentals** form the backbone of AI computation. Students must master vector and matrix operations, eigenvalues and eigenvectors for techniques like PCA, and understand how data points become vectors while datasets become matrices. **Calculus foundations** enable optimization understanding - derivatives measure change rates essential for gradient descent, while the chain rule provides the mathematical foundation for backpropagation. **Probability and statistics** concepts including Bayes' theorem, distributions, and statistical learning theory support uncertainty quantification and model evaluation.

### Learning paradigms and neural network basics

The **supervised learning framework** minimizes loss functions L(y, f(x)) between true labels y and predictions f(x), while **unsupervised learning** maximizes likelihood P(X|θ) to find hidden patterns. **Neural networks** implement the mathematical model: output = g(Σ(wi*xi) + b), where activation functions introduce crucial non-linearity. The **feedforward process** computes h^(l+1) = g(W^(l)h^(l) + b^(l)) as data flows through layers.

### Training fundamentals and generalization

**Backpropagation** efficiently computes gradients using the chain rule, enabling gradient descent updates: θ_new = θ_old - α∇L(θ). The **bias-variance tradeoff** balances model complexity with generalization - total error equals Bias² + Variance + Irreducible Error. **Regularization techniques** including L1/L2 penalties, dropout, and early stopping prevent overfitting while maintaining learning capacity.

## Module 2: Historical Context and Evolution (Weeks 5-6)

### Early foundations to neural networks

AI history begins with **McCulloch-Pitts neurons (1943)** and Hebb's learning rule (1949), progressing through the **perceptron era (1958)** and the first AI winter following Minsky and Papert's limitations analysis. The **backpropagation revolution (1986)** by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams enabled multilayer network training, overcoming single-layer perceptron constraints.

### Deep learning renaissance and transformer revolution

**AlexNet's 2012 ImageNet victory** launched modern deep learning with 15.3% error versus 26.2% for competitors, demonstrating deep CNN power. **Long Short-Term Memory (1995)** solved vanishing gradients for sequences, while **GPU acceleration** made training feasible. The **transformer architecture (2017)** eliminated recurrence entirely, replacing it with self-attention: Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T/√d_k)V, enabling full parallelization and superior long-range dependency modeling.

## Module 3: LLM Architecture and Technical Details (Weeks 7-10)

### Transformer architecture deep dive

**Modern LLMs** use decoder-only architectures with stacked transformer blocks. **Tokenization** converts text to subword units using Byte-Pair Encoding or SentencePiece, while **positional encoding** adds sequence information: PE(pos,2i) = sin(pos/10000^(2i/d_model)). **Multi-head self-attention** processes queries, keys, and values across h=8 parallel heads before concatenation and linear projection.

### Advanced architectural components

**Layer normalization** stabilizes training: LayerNorm(x) = γ * (x - μ)/σ + β, while **residual connections** enable gradient flow: output = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x)). **Feed-forward networks** expand and contract representations: FFN(x) = max(0, xW1 + b1)W2 + b2, typically using ReLU or GELU activations with d_ff = 4 * d_model dimensions.

### Scale and model families

**GPT family** uses autoregressive next-token prediction with causal masking, while **BERT family** employs masked language modeling for bidirectional understanding. **Model parameters** now range from 1B (efficient models) to trillions (frontier models), with **context windows** expanding from 2K to 10M+ tokens in latest releases like Llama 4.

## Module 4: Training Processes and Alignment (Weeks 11-14)

### Pre-training foundations

**Large-scale pre-training** uses causal language modeling: L = -∑(i=1 to n) log P(x_i | x_1, ..., x_{i-1}) on massive corpora of hundreds of billions to trillions of tokens. **Training infrastructure** requires thousands of GPUs/TPUs with sophisticated parallelization strategies including data, model, and pipeline parallelism, costing millions of dollars for frontier models.

### Fine-tuning and alignment techniques

**Supervised fine-tuning** adapts pre-trained models to follow instructions using high-quality prompt-response pairs. **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** implements a three-stage process: initial model preparation, reward model training using Bradley-Terry preference modeling, and policy optimization with PPO including KL divergence penalties to prevent drift.

### Constitutional AI and modern alignment

**Constitutional AI** represents a major advancement, using explicit principles to guide model behavior through self-critique and revision. **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** eliminates reward model complexity by optimizing directly on preference data with the loss function: L_DPO = -E[log σ(β log π_θ(y_w|x)/π_ref(y_w|x) - β log π_θ(y_l|x)/π_ref(y_l|x))]. **Parameter-efficient methods** like LoRA decompose weight updates into low-rank matrices, dramatically reducing trainable parameters.

## Knowledge acquisition and organization mastery

### The Zettelkasten method: Atomic knowledge building

Zettelkasten—German for "slip box"—revolutionizes how you build and discover knowledge. Instead of organizing information by subject, you create atomic notes (one concept per note) that link organically based on actual relationships.

Each note receives a unique identifier and focuses on a single idea you can state in one or two sentences. The power comes from linking—not categorizing. A note about "Milky Way visibility conditions" might link to astronomy notes, photography technique notes, travel planning notes, and atmospheric science notes, creating unexpected synthesis opportunities.

**Implement Zettelkasten principles** by writing notes in your own words, linking liberally during creation, and regularly browsing your note network without specific goals. This "intellectual wandering" often produces the most valuable insights.

### Progressive summarization: Layered understanding

Building on Tiago Forte's approach, progressive summarization creates increasingly refined understanding through multiple processing layers. Layer 1 captures original content with source attribution. Layer 2 bolds important passages during first reading. Layer 3 highlights the most crucial insights during review. Layer 4 creates executive summaries in your own words. Layer 5 adds personal commentary and connections to other knowledge.

This mirrors how Randall processes photographic scenes—initial capture, basic adjustments, careful selection of the most impactful elements, creative enhancement, and final integration with his broader artistic vision.

### PARA method integration

For project-oriented research, the PARA method (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive) provides complementary structure to Zettelkasten's network approach. Projects have deadlines and specific outcomes (like "Create astrophotography guide" or "Understand galaxy formation"). Areas represent ongoing responsibilities without endpoints (like "Photography skill development"). Resources support future reference (like "Lens testing methodologies"). Archives contain inactive items from other categories.

Use PARA for actionable organization while maintaining Zettelkasten for conceptual development. Your "Deep Sky Photography" project folder connects to Zettelkasten notes about specific techniques, equipment, and theoretical understanding.

## Source evaluation and information quality assessment

### The SIFT method for digital literacy

In an era of information abundance, the SIFT method provides systematic evaluation: **Stop** before using information to assess its value. **Investigate the source** by researching author credentials, publication reputation, and institutional affiliations. **Find alternative coverage** to verify claims and identify different perspectives. **Trace to original** sources to understand context and avoid misrepresentation.

For photography and cosmology research, this means checking photographer credentials and experience, verifying astronomical data against established databases, and tracing technique descriptions to original developers or scientific sources.

### Authority assessment in creative and scientific domains

Authority varies significantly between photography and cosmology. In photography, authority comes from demonstrated mastery (portfolio quality), teaching ability, and peer recognition. In cosmology, authority derives from academic credentials, peer review publications, and institutional affiliations.

Develop expertise in recognizing **constructed authority**—understanding that different communities value different types of evidence and expertise. A photographer's insights about composition may carry more weight than their opinions about astrophysics, just as a cosmologist's theoretical understanding might not translate to practical imaging skills.

### Evaluating interdisciplinary sources

Sources that bridge photography and cosmology require special evaluation criteria. Look for authors who demonstrate competence in both domains, cite appropriate sources from both fields, and acknowledge the limitations of their cross-disciplinary expertise.

Glenn Randall exemplifies this balanced approach—he clearly distinguishes between his mountaineering and photography expertise versus his astronomical knowledge, citing appropriate scientific sources for technical claims while speaking from experience about practical imaging challenges.
