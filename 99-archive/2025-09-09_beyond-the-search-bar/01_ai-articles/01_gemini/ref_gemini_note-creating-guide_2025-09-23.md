The visual design of your note is all about reducing friction and making the information as easy to absorb as possible. When you open a note in six months, you should be able to grasp its core message in seconds.

*   **Descriptive Title:** The title is the note's primary identity. It should be concise yet descriptive enough to be understood on its own. Instead of "Notes on a Book," use "Key Arguments in 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'."
*<mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">   **The Summary Block:** Right below the title, include a 1-2 sentence summary in your own words</mark>. This is the single most important part of a scannable note. It forces you to process the idea and provides immediate context for your future self.
*   **Clear Headings:** Use headings (##) and subheadings (###) to create a logical outline. This breaks up the wall of text and allows you to jump to relevant sections easily.
*   **Strategic Formatting:** Use formatting with clear purpose.
    *   **Bold:** For **key terms** and concepts. This helps them pop out.
    *   _Italics:_ For _emphasis_, book titles, or direct quotes.
    *   Highlights (if your app supports them): For actionable insights or questions you want to revisit.
*   **Bulleted & Numbered Lists:**
    *   Use bullet points (like this one) to group related, non-sequential ideas.
    *   Use numbered lists for step-by-step processes or sequential arguments.
*  <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;"> **White Space:** Don't be afraid of empty lines. White space gives your eyes and brain room to breathe, separating ideas and improving readability</mark>.

\# Spaced Repetition  
  
\> Spaced repetition is a learning technique that involves reviewing information at increasing intervals over time to move it from short-term to long-term memory.  
  
The core principle is to interrupt the natural process of forgetting. By revisiting a concept just as you are about to forget it, you strengthen its neural pathway.  
  
\## ## How It Works  
  
1\. **\*\*Initial Learning:\*\*** You learn a new piece of information.  
2\. **\*\*First Review:\*\*** You review it soon after, perhaps within a day.  
3\. **\*\*Increasing Intervals:\*\*** Each subsequent review happens after a longer period (e.g., 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month).  
  
This method is far more effective than "cramming" because it leverages the **\*\*testing effect\*\***, which shows that the act of retrieval itself strengthens memory. It stands in contrast to passive review, which is a less effective strategy discussed in \`\[\[Active Recall vs Passive Review\]\]\`.  
  
\## ## Key Benefits  
  
\* **\*\*Efficiency:\*\*** You spend less time reviewing things you already know well.  
\* **\*\*Durability:\*\*** The knowledge becomes deeply embedded in long-term memory.  
\* **\*\*Scalability:\*\*** It works for learning anything from languages to complex scientific concepts.
