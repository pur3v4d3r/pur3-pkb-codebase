# Vera Rubin (1928-2016)

> *"Stars don't care about the observer's gender" - The astronomer who revealed the invisible universe*

## ðŸŒŸ The Dark Matter Pioneer

Vera Cooper Rubin fundamentally changed our understanding of the universe by discovering compelling evidence for [[Dark Matter]] through her meticulous observations of galaxy rotation curves. Her work revealed that the visible universe is just a small fraction of what actually exists.

## ðŸ”¬ Scientific Contributions

### Galaxy Rotation Curves ([[Dark Matter]])
- **1970s Research**: Systematically measured rotation speeds of stars in spiral galaxies
- **Expected Result**: Stars farther from galactic center should orbit slower (Keplerian motion)
- **Shocking Discovery**: Stars at galaxy edges moved as fast as those near center
- **Implication**: **5x more mass present than visible** - the first strong evidence for dark matter

### Methodology Excellence  
- **Precision Instruments**: Used state-of-the-art spectrographs
- **Large Sample**: Studied dozens of galaxies systematically  
- **Collaboration**: Worked closely with Kent Ford on instrumentation
- **Reproducibility**: Results confirmed by multiple independent teams

### Key Publications
- **1970**: "Rotation of the Andromeda Nebula from a Spectroscopic Survey"  
- **1978**: "Extended rotation curves of high-luminosity spiral galaxies"
- **1980**: "Rotational properties of 21 SC galaxies" (with Ford)

## ðŸŽ“ Career and Background

### Early Life and Education
- **Born**: July 23, 1928, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania
- **Childhood**: Built telescope at age 14, tracked meteor showers
- **Vassar College**: BA in Astronomy (1948) - only astronomy major in her class
- **Cornell University**: MA in Astronomy (1951)
- **Georgetown University**: PhD in Astronomy (1954)

### Career Path
- **1965-2016**: Staff astronomer at Carnegie Institution's Department of Terrestrial Magnetism
- **Research Focus**: Galaxy dynamics, large-scale structure
- **Mentorship**: Guided numerous graduate students and postdocs

### Barriers Overcome
- **Gender Discrimination**: Excluded from Palomar Observatory until 1970s
- **Princeton**: Rejected from graduate program (didn't accept women until 1975)
- **Persistence**: Continued research despite systemic obstacles
- **Accommodation**: Had to arrange for separate bathroom facilities at observatories
