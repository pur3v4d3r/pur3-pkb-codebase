# Galaxy Formation

> *From quantum ripples to spiral galaxies - how tiny fluctuations became the cosmic structures we see today*

## üåå The Cosmic Construction Project

Galaxy formation is one of the universe's most complex and beautiful processes, transforming the nearly uniform early cosmos into the rich tapestry of galaxies, stars, and planets we observe today. It's the story of how [[Dark Matter]] and ordinary matter collaborated to build cosmic architecture from primordial seeds.

## üß± The Building Blocks

### Initial Conditions from [[Cosmic Inflation]]
- **Quantum Fluctuations**: Tiny density variations (Œ¥œÅ/œÅ ~ 10‚Åª‚Åµ)
- **Scale-Invariant**: Similar amplitude on all relevant scales
- **Gaussian**: Random, uncorrelated fluctuations
- **Preservation**: Frozen into [[Cosmic Microwave Background]] at recombination

### Dark Matter: The Scaffolding ([[Dark Matter]])
- **Dominant Component**: ~85% of all matter
- **Early Start**: Begins clustering immediately after [[Timeline of the Universe|recombination]]
- **Collisionless**: Doesn't interact with radiation, cools efficiently
- **Gravitational**: Only interacts via gravity - pure structure formation

### Ordinary Matter (Baryons): The Visible Component
- **Minority**: ~15% of total matter
- **Coupled**: Strongly interacted with photons until recombination
- **Delayed Start**: Couldn't cluster until universe became transparent
- **Complex Physics**: Cooling, heating, chemistry, star formation

## üïê Timeline of Structure Formation ([[Timeline of the Universe]])

### Phase 1: Dark Ages (380,000 - 100 million years)
- **Dark Matter Growth**: Halos begin forming via gravitational collapse
- **Baryon Dynamics**: Gas slowly falls into dark matter potential wells
- **First Structures**: Smallest scale fluctuations collapse first
- **No Light**: Universe is dark - no stars formed yet

### Phase 2: Cosmic Dawn (~100-200 million years)
- **First Stars**: Population III stars form in dark matter minihalos
- **Properties**: Massive (100-1000 M‚òâ), metal-free, short-lived
- **Impact**: First light since recombination, heavy element production
- **Beginning**: Start of stellar feedback and chemical enrichment

### Phase 3: Reionization (150 million - 1 billion years)
- **Ionizing Radiation**: First stars and quasars reionize universe
- **Feedback**: Radiation heats gas, suppresses small galaxy formation
- **Patchy Process**: Occurs in bubbles around first light sources
- **Observable**: 21cm hydrogen line signatures (future observations)

### Phase 4: Galaxy Assembly Era (1-10 billion years)
- **Peak Formation**: Most galaxies form during this period
- **Hierarchical**: Smaller structures merge to form larger ones
- **Morphology**: Disk and elliptical galaxies establish
- **Chemical Evolution**: Metal enrichment from stellar nucleosynthesis

### Phase 5: Modern Era (Last 5 billion years)
- **[[Dark Energy]] Domination**: Structure growth slows significantly
- **Mature Galaxies**: Most massive galaxies already formed
- **Star Formation Decline**: Universe becoming less active
- **Local Evolution**: Galaxy mergers, morphological transformation

## üî¨ Outstanding Questions

### The Core-Cusp Problem ([[The Dark Matter Problem]])
- **Simulations**: Predict central density cusps in dark matter halos
- **Observations**: Many galaxies show flattened central cores
- **Possible Solutions**: Baryonic feedback, self-interacting dark matter

### Missing Satellite Problem
- **Prediction**: Milky Way should have ~1000 satellite galaxies
- **Observation**: Only ~50 known satellite galaxies
- **Solutions**: Reionization suppression, warm dark matter

### Too-Big-to-Fail Problem  
- **Issue**: Some massive subhalos should host bright galaxies but don't
- **Implication**: Either missing physics or dark matter properties
- **Research**: Active area of theoretical and observational work

### Star Formation Efficiency
- **Question**: Why do galaxies convert only ~10% of gas to stars?
- **Feedback**: Stellar and AGN feedback regulate star formation
- **Observations**: Star formation rate decline over cosmic time
