### Topic 1: Historical Foundations and Theoretical Origins of SDT

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Understand the historical context that led to SDT's development (1971-1985)
> - Grasp the paradigm shift from behaviorism to organismic approaches
> - Identify key experiments and findings that established SDT's foundations
> - Contextualize SDT within the broader landscape of 20th-century psychology

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Foundational + Narrative-Driven**  
**Rationale**: Understanding SDT's origins requires both establishing conceptual building blocks and appreciating the historical narrative of how Deci and Ryan challenged prevailing behaviorist assumptions. A narrative approach brings the theory's development to life while foundational framing ensures clear conceptual scaffolding.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What empirical findings led Deci to question the behaviorist assumption that rewards enhance motivation?
2. How did the cognitive revolution in psychology create space for SDT's organismic perspective?
3. What were the key turning points between Deci's 1971 experiments and the 1985 formalization of SDT?
4. How does SDT's meta-theory differ from other motivation theories of its era?

**PKB Capture Strategy**:
- Core concepts to wiki-link: [[Intrinsic Motivation]], [[Extrinsic Motivation]], [[Organismic Theory]], [[Behaviorism]], [[Cognitive Revolution]], [[Edward Deci]], [[Richard Ryan]], [[Undermining Effect]]
- Recommended tags: #historical-context #theory-origins #paradigm-shift
- Connection points: Links to [[History of Psychology]], [[Motivational Psychology]], [[Human Development Theory]]

**Prerequisites**: Basic familiarity with psychological research methods and 20th-century psychology schools of thought

### Topic 2: The Three Basic Psychological Needs - Foundational Architecture

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Define autonomy, competence, and relatedness as psychological "nutrients"
> - Distinguish between need satisfaction, need deprivation, and need thwarting
> - Understand the universal vs. culturally-specific nature of basic needs
> - Examine empirical evidence for the necessity of each need

**Pedagogical Approach**: **First-Principles + Analytical**  
**Rationale**: The three basic needs function as axioms in SDT - everything else builds from here. First-principles reasoning helps you understand WHY these specific needs matter, while analytical examination of the empirical evidence develops critical evaluation skills.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What constitutes "autonomy" in SDT, and how does it differ from independence or individualism?
2. Why are these three needs considered "basic" and "universal" rather than learned or culturally variable?
3. What happens psychologically when each need is satisfied vs. thwarted vs. deprived?
4. How do the three needs interact and support each other?

**PKB Capture Strategy**:
- Core concepts to wiki-link: [[Autonomy]], [[Competence]], [[Relatedness]], [[Basic Psychological Needs]], [[Need Satisfaction]], [[Need Thwarting]], [[Need Frustration]], [[Eudaimonic Well-being]]
- Recommended tags: #basic-needs #theoretical-foundations #psychological-nutrients

**Prerequisites**: Topic 1 (Historical Foundations)

### Topic 3: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation - The Core Distinction

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Articulate the fundamental difference between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation
> - Understand the "undermining effect" of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation
> - Examine conditions under which extrinsic motivation can support (rather than undermine) autonomy
> - Apply the intrinsic/extrinsic distinction to real-world scenarios

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Expository + Case Studies**  
**Rationale**: This topic requires clear, precise explanation of a nuanced distinction, making expository teaching ideal. Case studies ground the abstract concepts in concrete scenarios, helping you recognize these motivation types in diverse contexts.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What makes an activity intrinsically motivating vs. extrinsically regulated?
2. Under what conditions do rewards undermine intrinsic motivation, and when might they support it?
3. How does the quality of motivation (not just quantity) affect performance, persistence, and well-being?
4. What role does perceived locus of causality play in the intrinsic/extrinsic distinction?

**PKB Capture Strategy**:
- Core concepts to wiki-link: [[Intrinsic Motivation]], [[Extrinsic Motivation]], [[Undermining Effect]], [[Overjustification Effect]], [[Perceived Locus of Causality]], [[Autonomous Motivation]], [[Controlled Motivation]]

**Prerequisites**: Topics 1-2

### Topic 4: The Six Mini-Theories - SDT's Theoretical Ecosystem

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Master all six mini-theories: Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET), Organismic Integration Theory (OIT), Causality Orientations Theory (COT), Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT), Goal Contents Theory (GCT), and Relationships Motivation Theory (RMT)
> - Understand how each mini-theory addresses specific motivational phenomena
> - Recognize when to apply each mini-theory to analyze motivational contexts
> - Synthesize the mini-theories into a coherent whole

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Systems Analysis + Analytical**  
**Rationale**: The six mini-theories form an interconnected ecosystem, making systems thinking essential. Analytical examination helps you understand the unique contribution of each theory while seeing how they integrate.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. What specific motivational question does each mini-theory address?
2. How does CET explain the effects of external events on intrinsic motivation?
3. What is the internalization continuum in OIT, and how do different regulation types emerge?
4. How do causality orientations (COT) reflect individual differences in motivational tendencies?
5. Why does the content of goals (GCT) matter as much as the reasons for pursuing them?
6. How does RMT apply SDT to close relationships and attachment?

**PKB Capture Strategy**:
- Core concepts to wiki-link: [[Cognitive Evaluation Theory]], [[Organismic Integration Theory]], [[Causality Orientations Theory]], [[Basic Psychological Needs Theory]], [[Goal Contents Theory]], [[Relationships Motivation Theory]], [[Internalization]], [[External Regulation]], [[Introjected Regulation]], [[Identified Regulation]], [[Integrated Regulation]], [[Intrinsic Goals]], [[Extrinsic Goals]], [[Autonomy Orientation]], [[Control Orientation]], [[Impersonal Orientation]]

### Topic 7: Comparative Analysis - SDT vs. Competing Motivation Theories

> [!definition] Learning Objectives
> - Compare and contrast SDT with major alternative motivation theories
> - Understand SDT's relationship to behaviorism, cognitive theories, and humanistic psychology
> - Examine points of compatibility and conflict with frameworks like Goal-Setting Theory, Achievement Goal Theory, Expectancy-Value Theory, and Flow Theory
> - Develop nuanced understanding of when SDT offers unique explanatory power

**Pedagogical Approach**: **Dialectical + Analytical**  
**Rationale**: Deep understanding emerges from examining competing perspectives. Dialectical reasoning helps you explore thesis-antithesis-synthesis dynamics between SDT and other frameworks, while analytical thinking develops critical evaluation skills.

**Key Questions to Explore**:
1. How does SDT's organismic approach differ from behaviorist and social-cognitive perspectives?
2. Where do SDT and Goal-Setting Theory agree and diverge on the role of goals in motivation?
3. How does SDT relate to Csikszentmihalyi's Flow Theory, and where do they intersect?
4. What critiques have been leveled against SDT, and how have Deci and Ryan responded?
